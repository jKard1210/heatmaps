Because of this, we decided to create a tool for better understanding the impact of inflation changes on different companies. We built a multi-layered Inflation Model that outputs an ‘Inflation Risk’ score to public U.S. equities. This score is intended to estimate how much each stock has been positively or negatively affected by changes in inflation expectations.

 

In this newsletter, we’ll outline how to read the dataset and reveal some of the initial findings uncovered using it.

Inflation Risk Methodology & Output

Below is a visual representation of the 2-layered simple regression model used to develop the dataset: 

The first layer is the CAPM Model Beta in which beta coefficients are passed into the linear model. The second layer processes the output from the CAPM stage with inflation (CPI) and personal consumption data (PCE), and uses another linear regression to predict the expected returns of the company.

The output assigns two values to every ticker:

 

Inflation Risk: The overall threat and/or exposure a stock movement has with inflation. Stocks with higher values are likely to have been more negatively affected by inflation, whereas stocks with lower scores have shown a more positive reaction to inflation. Below is a rubric showing how to interpret this risk score:

Confidence Interval: The conviction towards the assigned Inflation Score. The confidence score ranges from 0 to 1, with the higher the value representing a greater likelihood the Inflation Risk Value is significant. Scores vary based on the strength of correlation found by the regression model.

This model is based upon "Inflation and the Cross Section of Stock Returns" by Fernando Duerte (link). Armed with this knowledge, let’s apply it to the most recently published data and explore the findings below.

Inflation Risk Industry & Stock Analysis

Reviewing the graph, we see that real-estate, and commodity-based industries are expected to perform better during inflationary periods, whereas technology and growth industries have more risk exposure. Historically tangible assets like commodities have been seen as inflation hedges to investors as inflation and commodities typically share a close price correlation. For instance during the 1973 Oil Crisis, in which OPEC led an oil embargo on the United States, Crude Oil and the U.S. Dollar surged over 300% and 11% respectively. Similar occurrences of this would continue to repeat with the 1979 Oil Crisis, and 2008 Financial Crisis.

Analyzing the data at a company level, users can potentially use the Inflation Risk metric to help find industry outliers. To demonstrate this, we filtered the data to only include software & IT companies, with low inflation risk metrics and confidence intervals:

Looking at the “% Change YTD” column illustrations that these companies have outperformed their industry peers with an average return of 36.7% YTD, compared to QQQ returning 16.90% in the same period. Conversely if you believed inflation were to level off, high Inflation Risk companies could potentially outperform their low inflation risk competitors.
