Introducing two new alternative metrics to make sense of patent data

Technological Drift and Momentum

In our last newsletter, we introduced our new U.S. patent dataset which scrapes USPTO patent grants, and maps them to public companies, helping investors identify opportunities and trends within companies and or industries. 

 

Unfortunately, patents are long, often esoteric, and thousands of them are granted every week. This makes it very difficult for investors to extract meaningful insights from a company's raw patent data. To help solve this we have started publishing two new metrics, Tech Drift and Tech Momentum, that quantify different elements of a company's patent history into a single number. Here is a brief summary:

 

Tech Drift

Companies that are undergoing a period of technological change are likely to behave (and possibly perform), differently than those which are keeping a stable research focus. Tech Drift is a metric we created to quantify the extent to which a company is changing its R&D focus, and beginning to explore new fields. 

 

Tech Momentum

Industry momentum has been used for decades by investors to track trends across baskets of similar companies. However, industry momentum fails to account for technological links that occur across industry lines (and misses out on trends like the 2017 blockchain craze). Tech Momentum, first introduced in 2019, addresses this problem by creating a momentum factor based on recent performance of a company's tech peers. 

     

In this newsletter, we're going to explain the methodology behind the new metrics and how they can be used to extract important information from patent data and identify potential investment opportunities.

Tech Drift

Tech drift can be briefly summarized as a numerical value that weighs firms based on how much they're exploring entirely new sectors of research, rather than focusing on existing areas of expertise.

 

To quantify this, we looked at the IPC codes assigned to each of a company's patents. The IPC classification system is used by the US Patent Office to assign patents to a general technological category. There are currently 427 different patent classes under this system, with frequent revisions to account for new areas of innovation.

Using these IPC codes, we can construct a "patent profile" for each company, showing what proportion of its recent patents belong to each patent class. We can then track changes to this profile each month, and measure the extent to which a company is changing its technological focus.

Tech Momentum

Companies with closely aligned technological expertise often have similar outside factors that affect their firm’s value. Companies with technological links often share supply chains and are similarly affected by certain market shocks. Because these links often exist across industry lines, the market may be slow to recognize these effects, which could result in lag-lead effects between technologically similar firms.

 

Instead of searching for technological links on a case-by-case basis, we can use patent profiles (as described in the previous section) to systematically determine the closeness between every pair of firms. We can then use this closeness metric to find a technological momentum factor for every firm.

 

From this, we can quantify a Tech Momentum value for patent-producing companies, with higher values being indicative of strong performance by a companies' technological peers in the previous quarter.

This practice can help identify the hottest and coldest tech sectors, and see which companies that are over or underperforming their tech peers.

Conclusion

In this newsletter, we introduced two metrics that can be used to interpret and quantify complex patent data. Tech Drift measures the extent to which a company is shifting its research focus, while Tech Momentum looks at the recent performance of a company's technological peers.
